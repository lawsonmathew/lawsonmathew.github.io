High Altitude Sonic Anemometer Payload

Final design and layout

In my senior design class at Tufts University, I worked on a semester long project to help a client with their problem. The problem was to show the feasability of the Tufts sonic anemometer, a windspeed measuring instrument, at a high altitude environment by building a balloon payload that will store the data from the anemometer to an sd card for analysis when recovered. In order to make sense of the anemometer data, storing the output of a variety of sensors like temperature, pressure, gps, and magnetometer with a synced clock was necessary. Furthermore, we had to building an insulating enclosure for the electronics as well as a mount for the anemometer.

I was leading the group making sure that all the deadlines were met, meetings were scheduled, and all parts of the project were coming together. I focused on programming all the sensors to store its data to an sd card on the Raspberry Pi Zero W and assembly of the whole project. The following paragraph describes the timeline of events during the semester in which all the group members helped to complete.

Initially, we started with some concept generation like using morphology tables and a systems diagram and then moved on to finalizing a components list which included batteries to use. After buying electronic parts, decisions about materials and final designs of the enclosure and mount were made. One of the main objectives was that the whole payload had to weigh under 5 pounds and was successfully achieved. Data logging validation was completed as well. In the end, we did a poster presentation to show of our final prototype and handed over our project for the client to use.

Group members: Harris Hardiman-Mastow, Benjamic McDermott, Will Yorton, Shreyansh Agarwal

IMechE Line Launcher Challenge

CAD model and final prototype

During my study abroad at University College London, I participated in a group project where a squash ball launcher had to be fabricated. It was built according to the IMechE Line Launcher Challenge rules of which the main purpose was to have an automated line launcher which fired at targets of three different heights.

I led the team by organizing meetings and helping to assign parts to be completed by the members of the group. The year long project was split into two parts, one was creating the final design and the second was the assembly of the launcher. I worked on some parts of the CAD modelling using CATIA as well as the assembly of the automated launcher. This was my first exposure to the Arduino and other microcontrollers so my computer science experience helped with the programming of the motors and general function. I also gained more experience with laser cutting and 3D printing during this project.

The final prototype worked effectively at launching the ball but didn't achieve the goal of hitting the targets. But through this project, I embraced becoming a leader and worked on improving my leadership skills. I also gained technical knowledge and was able to put my engineering skills to create a product from scratch.

Animation: Fisson Theory of the Moon

During my graphic arts course, I decided to make this animation for a presentation on moon formation theories and the fission theory stuck out to me. I made this animation to show what the fission theory was and what happened to the earth and moon during it. Using Autodesk 3ds Max and a video editing software, I modeled, rendered and edited the animation into the final product you see above.

What I learned from my previous encounters with 3D modeling softwares was the difficulty of working with different perspectives and that project of this size is arduous. I improved on my skills of working with 3D modeling softwares as I worked on this project. The animation wasn't perfect, but I was able to create different parts of the simulation with the help of set ideas that it needed to fulfill. The overall experience was fulfilling because I was able to come up with a good end product.

Virtual Tour at the Shedd Aquarium

I, along with my fellow members in the Tech Genius club at my highschool, worked with the Teen Lab at the Shedd Aquarium to make a virtual reality tour for the Amazon Rising Exhibit. This project took over a semester and we used a 360 camera to make this work. I spent most of the time with helping with ideation and the organization of the project, and working on the scripts for the commentary in the videos.

I learned a lot about teamwork and how I fit into collaborative situations. There was also some presentations that was done to showcase our project, which helped me become a better public speaker. The overall project helped me build better connections with others and improve upon the skills I had and wanted to develop further.
